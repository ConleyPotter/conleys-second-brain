# The Whole System Is Designed Around Limits, Not Targets

**Bridge:** ACE's 15-hour ceiling, fibromyalgia's hard upper bounds, burnout-as-invalidation-condition, and the ACE pivot all point to the same design philosophy: every element of the operating system is an architectural response to non-negotiable limits rather than a willpower response to aspirational targets.

**Potential hook:** "The thing that makes this system work isn't discipline — it's physics. I don't manage my hours because I'm organized. I built a pipeline that fits inside 15 hours because it's the only way the math works. I don't 'manage' fibromyalgia. I design systems that function at its limits. Burnout isn't a risk to mitigate; it's a failure condition that cancels the year. Most people optimize for what they want. I optimize for what cannot be exceeded."

**Why this matters:** The ACE pivot is usually framed as a strategic correction — it was "a physics correction" because the original system violated the 15-hour ceiling by design. The fibromyalgia note observes that it and the 15-hour cap "function identically" as hard upper bounds. Burnout isn't described as a risk in the operating doctrine — it's an invalidation condition, a system state. Taken together, these aren't separate accommodations: they're a coherent engineering philosophy. The system isn't designed around what Conley wants to achieve; it's designed around what cannot be exceeded without breaking something irreversible. This is the opposite of hustle-culture design, which treats limits as temporary and to be overcome. Here, limits are the design brief. The non-obvious content opportunity is naming this: **most people set goals and try to exceed them; this system sets hard limits and tries to stay inside them**. The goal is a downstream output of the limit-respecting architecture, not a direct target.
