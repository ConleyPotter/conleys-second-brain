# **Executive Summary**

The River Room is a community-rooted creative commons and digital workshop to be established in a restored historic building in Marietta, Pennsylvania. It will serve as a third place — a warm, welcoming anchor that blends a curated café and bookshop, creative studio membership, and an accessible AI and digital skills co-op for artists, writers, local businesses, and the broader Susquehanna Valley community.

Marietta is a small river town with historic bones, a growing creative class, proximity to the Appalachian Trail corridor, and a distinct lack of dedicated community programming space. The River Room is designed to fill that gap — not as a commercial venue, but as a place of genuine purpose.

| The Core Concept Three interlocking layers: a café and bookshop open to all (The Commons), a membership-based creative studio for writers, artists, and makers (The Studio), and an accessible digital and AI skills co-op for creatives and small business owners (The Workshop). Together they create a self-sustaining ecosystem — the front draws people in, the studio builds community, and the workshop generates meaningful recurring revenue. |
| :---- |

This business plan details the vision, market opportunity, operating model, programming, financial projections, and phased launch strategy for The River Room. It is designed to be fundable through a combination of personal investment, small business loans, Pennsylvania arts grants, and community crowdfunding.

# **Market Opportunity**

## **The Community Context**

Marietta (pop. \~2,600) sits at the intersection of several powerful dynamics that make this concept well-timed:

* Lancaster County continues to attract remote workers, artists, and entrepreneurs priced out of larger metros.

* The AT corridor and Susquehanna River drive meaningful trail tourism — hikers, cyclists, and paddlers who need amenities, community, and a place to linger.

* There is no dedicated creative co-working or workshop space in Marietta or the immediate surrounding area.

* Local tradespeople and small businesses are under-served digitally — a known gap given the AutoBoost client base in the region.

* Pennsylvania has robust arts and cultural grant infrastructure through the Pennsylvania Council on the Arts and county-level cultural trusts.

| Audience | Description | Primary Touchpoint |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Local Creatives | Writers, artists, photographers, designers within 20-mile radius | Studio membership, workshops, residency |

## **The Competitive Landscape**

There is no direct competitor within Marietta or Columbia Borough. The nearest analogues — coffee shops with community space, makerspaces, or co-working venues — are 15–25 miles away in Lancaster City. The River Room occupies an open and distinctive niche: the intersection of creative culture, digital accessibility, and community belonging in a small river town setting.

# **The Space**

## **The Building**

The ideal River Room building is a historic commercial structure in downtown Marietta — brick construction, original windows, high ceilings, and the kind of material memory that newer buildings cannot manufacture. Marietta's historic district has several such structures, including former bank buildings, merchant storefronts, and row commercial properties. The target is 3,000–5,000 sq ft across two floors, with a basement or rear addition for equipment and storage.

| Why Restoration Matters The act of restoring a historic building in a small town is itself a statement of values. It says: this place is worth preserving. The bones of 19th-century commerce become the container for 21st-century community. The River Room's aesthetic is not manufactured — it is inherited. |
| :---- |

# **Programming & Services**

## **The Commons — Daily Operations**

* Café service: specialty coffee, tea, simple food (pastries, sandwiches sourced locally). Not a full restaurant — a thoughtful café with a kitchen-confident hand behind it.

* Curated bookshop: literature, Appalachian & regional history, nature writing, technology & AI for non-engineers, recovery and spirituality, local authors prominently featured.

* Open seating daily. No time limits. Wifi. A place you are invited to stay.

* Local art on the walls — rotating quarterly, 20% commission to artists on sales.

## **The Workshop — Digital & AI Co-op**

The Workshop is the engine of earned revenue and community impact. It translates complex digital tools into practical power for people who don't consider themselves technical.

**Recurring Workshop Series**

* AI for Humans (monthly, 2-hr): Prompt craft, creative AI tools, building without coding. For writers, artists, and small business owners.

* Digital Clinic (bi-weekly, drop-in): One-on-one office hours. Website help, social media strategy, automation basics. Co-branded with AutoBoost.

* Tools Not Toys (quarterly intensive): A half-day workshop on integrating AI into a creative practice or small business without losing your voice or identity.

* River Writers Workshop (monthly): Craft-focused writing group with optional digital publishing component — how to get your work into the world.

* Nature Journal & Field Sketching (seasonal): Outdoors on the Susquehanna, connects to conservation partnerships.

* Zine Lab (monthly): Design and print a small publication. Risograph or laser. Community storytelling.

**Corporate & Organizational Training**

Packaged workshops offered to local employers, nonprofits, and regional organizations. 3-hour to full-day formats. AI literacy, digital marketing fundamentals, creative thinking for teams. This is the highest-margin revenue stream.

**Speaker & Community Programming**

* Monthly community conversations: climate, conservation, local history, recovery, creativity. Free or suggested donation.

* Quarterly author readings tied to bookshop inventory.

* Annual River Room Creative Summit: a one-day gathering of makers, writers, and digital thinkers from across the Susquehanna Valley.

# **Financial Plan**

The River Room operates a dual financial model: a physical business (the café, bookshop, studio, and workshop) and a parallel digital revenue operation (evergreen products, online membership, cohort courses, consulting, and sponsorship). These two operations are distinct legal and accounting entities but share a unified mission — the digital business funds and de-risks the physical one. Full detail on the digital revenue architecture is contained in the companion Digital Revenue Strategy document.

| The Strategic Shift The original business plan modeled the River Room as a physical-only operation dependent on foot traffic, grants, and local programming revenue. The addition of a parallel digital revenue strategy fundamentally changes the risk profile: the physical space no longer needs to be profitable from day one. It needs to be viable — covering its own operating costs — while the digital operation generates the surplus capital that funds growth, renovation, and mission expansion. |

## **Funding Strategy**

The addition of the digital revenue operation modifies the funding strategy in two ways: startup capital requirements remain similar, but the working capital reserve needs are lower because the digital business begins generating revenue before the physical building opens. Grant dependency in Years 2–3 drops significantly as digital revenue scales.

* Personal investment / founder equity: $30,000 – $50,000

* SBA 7(a) small business loan: $80,000 – $150,000

* Digital revenue pre-accumulation (Years 1–2 before physical opening): $40,000 – $80,000 directed to building fund

* Pennsylvania Council on the Arts — Cultural Facility Grant: $15,000 – $40,000

* Lancaster County Community Foundation: $5,000 – $20,000

* Community crowdfunding (Kickstarter or Local): $10,000 – $25,000 — the online audience makes this campaign significantly more viable

* Historic preservation tax credits (if applicable to chosen building): reduces effective restoration cost by up to 20%

| The Year 3 Threshold If by the end of Year 3 the digital operation is not generating at least $75,000 annually, a formal strategic review is triggered. Options include: accelerating the marketing plan, reconsidering location, pursuing additional grant capital, or adjusting the physical space's cost structure. This threshold is not a failure condition — it is a decision trigger built into the plan to protect both the mission and the founder's financial health. |

# **Phased Launch Strategy**

The River Room does not require a perfect building or a fully built-out space to begin. Community is built before walls.

## **Phase 0 — Proof of Concept (Months 1–6)**

Before acquiring a building, establish the programming and the community. This phase costs almost nothing and proves the concept.

* Host River Room pop-up workshops in borrowed spaces (libraries, churches, partner venues).

* Launch a newsletter and minimal social presence. Begin building the audience.

* Run 2–3 AI for Humans workshops and a River Writers session. Charge a modest fee. See who shows up.

* Begin the building search in parallel. Engage a local commercial real estate agent with historic property experience.

* Apply for initial grants in this phase — Pennsylvania arts grants open on a rolling basis.

## **Phase 1 — Open the Commons (Months 7–18)**

Lease or acquire the building. Open the café and bookshop. Begin soft programming.

* Restoration and build-out in phases — Commons first, Studio and Workshop to follow.

* Soft launch the café. Lean on food and beverage to drive daily foot traffic.

* Begin regular programming: monthly community night, writer's group, first AI workshop series.

* Launch studio memberships with a founding member rate and early-adopter perks.

* Establish local press presence: LNP Lancaster, Harrisburg Patriot-News, regional arts outlets.

## **Phase 2 — Activate the Workshop (Months 12–24)**

With foot traffic established, formalize the Workshop and digital co-op.

* Open the AI and digital workshop space with a launch event.

* Launch the corporate training offering — one anchor client (could be an AutoBoost relationship) to start.

* Hire a part-time Studio Coordinator to free the founder for higher-leverage programming.

* Establish the first annual River Room Creative Summit.

* Pursue second-year grant funding based on demonstrated community impact metrics.

## **Phase 3 — Deepen & Sustain (Year 3+)**

The River Room is no longer a startup — it is an institution.

* Evaluate transition to worker cooperative or hybrid nonprofit model if aligned with community needs.

* Expand residency programming with named fellowships and partnered funding.

* Consider a satellite model: could a second River Room exist in Columbia, Elizabethtown, or another river town?

* Produce and distribute a River Room zine or chapbook series — archiving the community's creative output.

# **Risk Analysis & Mitigation**

| Risk | Description | Mitigation |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Building acquisition delays | Historic properties carry renovation risk and timeline uncertainty | Phase 0 is building-independent; begin programming immediately |
| Slow café ramp-up | Food & beverage is competitive and margin-thin | Keep menu simple; focus on quality over complexity; café is a draw, not the core |
| Workshop under-enrollment | New programming takes time to find its audience | Phase 0 pilot workshops prove demand before capital commitment |
| Founder burnout | Multi-role operations risk is real, especially in Year 1 | Hire café lead early; build volunteer program; founder focus on programming & vision |
| Grant dependency | Grant funding is never guaranteed year-to-year | Revenue model is designed to reach sustainability without grants; grants accelerate, not sustain |
| AI hype backlash | Community skepticism about AI is real and valid | Tools Not Toys framing directly addresses this; humanism-first positioning is the differentiator |

# **Why This. Why Here. Why Now.**

Marietta is a town that remembers what it was. The brick buildings downtown, the river a block away, the sense that something important happened here and could again — this is not nostalgia. It is the right foundation for something that matters.

The River Room is not a business idea born of market research. It was born of a walk past a beautiful old building and a simple thought: what if this became something good? It is also the convergence of an unusual set of life experiences — decades in restaurants, years in digital marketing, a systems architect's mind, an English literature graduate's love of the written word, a hiker's relationship with the land, and a spiritually grounded understanding of what community actually requires.

Technology is accelerating faster than most people can absorb. Creativity is under pressure. Small towns are losing their third places. And the need for genuine, human community — the kind where you show up and are known — has never been more acute.

The River Room is the answer to all of that, in one building, in one river town, built by one person who has lived enough to know what restoration actually looks and feels like.
