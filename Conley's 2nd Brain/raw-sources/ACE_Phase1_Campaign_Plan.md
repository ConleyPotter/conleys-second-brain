## 1. Campaign Overview

### What This Campaign Is

Phase I of ACE needs one thing: enough paid contracts to fund 6–12 months of operating runway. This campaign is not about brand awareness at scale, viral content, or follower counts. It's about acquiring the first 5–10 verified clients on Upwork, turning those into reviews and case study material, and building the infrastructure — profile, portfolio, email list — to compound from there.

**Primary Objective:** Secure 5 completed Upwork contracts with 5-star reviews within 90 days of campaign launch.

**Secondary Objectives:**
- Establish a portfolio website that converts cold traffic into profile visits or inquiries
- Build a 30-post Threads archive that documents the building-in-public journey (audience growth is secondary; content library is the goal)
- Set up a simple email retention sequence for first clients
- Activate Fiverr as a secondary acquisition channel (lower priority, same deliverable)

### One-Sentence Summary

Get the first clients, earn the first reviews, build the credibility flywheel — while protecting the day job and staying inside 15 hours a week.

## 2. Target Audience

### Primary: Upwork Buyers — B2B Agencies and Sales Teams

**Profile:** Marketing agencies, fractional CMOs, RevOps consultants, and SDR teams with a quota and no research budget. They're posting on Upwork because hiring a full-time data analyst is overkill. They need verified contacts — specific titles, specific geographies, specific industries — delivered fast, in a format their CRM can ingest.

**Pain points:**
- Lead lists from ZoomInfo or Apollo are expensive and often stale
- In-house research takes SDR time away from selling
- They've been burned by garbage CSVs from overseas contractors who scraped LinkedIn with a browser extension
- They don't care how the list was built. They care that it works.

**Where they are:** Upwork search results, project catalog, and — eventually — profile referrals from first clients.

**Buying stage:** Decision-ready. They're on Upwork because they've already decided to outsource. The proposal is the conversion event.

---

### Secondary: Threads — Freelancers, Indie Builders, Systems Thinkers

**Profile:** People building something on the side. Freelancers, solopreneurs, developers shipping automation tools, people chasing financial independence. They don't need B2B lead enrichment — they're watching the process.

**Why they matter:** This audience doesn't buy from ACE. They amplify the content, follow the journey, and eventually become the referral network for Phase II and Phase III. The Threads strategy for Phase I is to plant seeds, not harvest.

**Buying stage:** Not applicable. This is a brand-building and relationship-building audience.

---

### Audience Separation Protocol

This campaign runs two parallel narratives that must never cross:

| Channel | Audience | What They Know |
|---|---|---|
| Upwork / Fiverr / Portfolio | B2B buyers | A skilled lead research specialist who delivers verified CSVs |
| Threads | Builders / Freelancers | Someone building systems-driven freelance work in the open |
| LinkedIn | AutoBoost ecosystem | A digital marketing executive building a B2B marketing automation product |
| Email | Past / active clients | A reliable service provider who checks in |

The automation stack — n8n, Apollo, Apify, Supabase — is never disclosed to clients, in any channel. The Threads content can reference "building systems" and "automating workflows" in broad strokes, but stops well short of the operational detail in the internal brief.

## 3. Key Messages

### Core Message (Client-Facing)
> "Verified B2B contact data, structured for your CRM, built around the exact titles and geographies you actually need."

This message does three things: it addresses quality (verified), format (structured for your CRM), and specificity (exact titles and geographies). It says nothing about how the list is built.

---

### Supporting Messages by Channel

**Upwork / Fiverr Profile and Proposals**

- *Quality signal:* "Every contact is verified against live data before delivery. You get a clean CSV, not a scrape."
- *Speed signal:* "Most 500-contact lists are delivered within 48–72 hours of order confirmation."
- *Specificity signal:* "I build lists around the titles you actually target — not generic job categories. If you need Plant Directors and VP Ops at mid-size manufacturers in Pennsylvania, that's what you get."
- *Risk reduction:* "Fixed price. No scope creep. You know what you're getting before we start."

**Portfolio Website**

- *Credibility:* Show the output, not the process. Sample CSV structure, before/after data quality example, anonymized client use case.
- *Positioning:* B2B data specialist with a focus on industrial, manufacturing, and regional markets in the mid-Atlantic.
- *CTA:* "See my Upwork profile" or "Send me a brief" — drive to wherever conversion happens.

**Threads (Building-in-Public)**

- *Stakes framing:* Why financial independence matters, what the freelance arbitrage model actually looks like when you run it at 15 hours a week.
- *Process documentation:* The tension between building something and having enough time to build it. What breaks. What's working.
- *Honest hunger:* "I'm not rushing to quit my job. But I'm not stopping either."

---

### Message Hierarchy for Upwork Proposals

1. **Why should I care?** Your SDR team is spending time on research instead of selling.
2. **What is the solution?** A verified, structured lead list, built to your exact targeting criteria.
3. **Why me?** Fixed price, fast turnaround, clean output — no revisions, no scope creep.
4. **What should I do?** Accept the proposal. I'll send a one-question intake form to confirm targeting parameters.

## 4. Channel Strategy

### Channel 1: Upwork (Primary)

**Why this channel:** Phase I objective is contract acquisition. Upwork is where B2B buyers with immediate need and allocated budget post jobs. The ACE pipeline already generates draft proposals automatically — the operator reviews and submits. That makes Upwork the highest ROI channel by far: the pipeline does the prospecting, and the operator contributes 2 minutes per submission.

**Strategy:**
- Optimize the profile before any proposals go out (see Next Steps)
- Build the profile for credibility signals Upwork's algorithm rewards: response rate, profile completeness, portfolio samples
- Set initial rate slightly below market ($350–450 for a 500-contact list) to win the first 2–3 contracts and accumulate reviews; raise rate after 5 reviews
- Target job posts with budgets of $300–700 and clear deliverable scope ("lead list," "contact list," "B2B data")
- Do not apply to vague "lead generation" posts that imply ongoing outreach — that's out of scope for Phase I

**Content format:** Profile bio, specialized profile for lead enrichment/B2B data, proposal templates (Spartan Tone Protocol), portfolio samples (anonymized CSV previews, methodology overview)

**Estimated effort:** 3–4 hrs/week (1 hr profile/optimization, 2–3 hrs proposal review and manual submission)

**Success signal:** 5 contracts with 5-star reviews before Day 90

---

### Channel 2: Personal Portfolio Website (Supporting)

**Why this channel:** Upwork profiles don't rank in Google. A portfolio site creates a credibility anchor outside of Upwork — one that can be linked in proposals, shared in cold outreach if Phase II requires it, and used to build legitimacy with higher-ticket clients in later phases. Phase I requires a minimum viable portfolio: one page, one service, one CTA.

**Strategy:**
- Build a single-page site focused entirely on B2B lead enrichment (not ACE as a brand, not Conley-as-full-stack-freelancer)
- Include: a short professional bio, the service offering, what the deliverable looks like, a process overview (non-technical), and a clear CTA to Upwork or a contact form
- Add a sample portfolio item: an anonymized, partially redacted example CSV with a brief on what targeting parameters it was built against
- The site should function as a secondary conversion path, not a primary one in Phase I

**Content format:** Single-page website (or simple multi-page: Home, Service, Portfolio, Contact)

**Estimated effort:** 4–6 hrs to build (one-time), 1 hr/month to maintain

**Success signal:** Site live before first proposal batch goes out; linked in Upwork bio

---

### Channel 3: Threads (Brand Building)

**Why this channel:** Threads is where the building-in-public audience lives right now. It's lower-friction than LinkedIn for personal content, and it doesn't carry the professional context risk that LinkedIn does for Conley (protecting the day job). Phase I Threads content is not about client acquisition — it's about planting the narrative: who Conley is, what he's building, why it matters.

**Strategy:**
- Post 3x/week: one post documenting the ACE build process (system design, workflow decisions, what broke), one post about the financial independence pursuit (stakes, economics, tension), one post that's more personal/reflective (gym, fibromyalgia, the marriage-ambition tension)
- Write in Conley's brand voice: grounded, specific, no hustle culture performance
- Don't pitch. Don't CTA. Document.
- Operational boundary: never name clients, never reveal the automation stack, never post anything that reveals ACE's arbitrage model to prospective clients

**Content format:** Short-form posts (200–400 words), occasional image/graphic if a workflow diagram or data visualization supports the story

**Estimated effort:** 2–3 hrs/week (drafting, scheduling, light engagement)

**Success signal:** 30-post archive by Day 90; at least one post per week that documents the ACE build process specifically

---

### Channel 4: LinkedIn (AutoBoost Only)

**Why this channel:** Conley's LinkedIn presence is already positioned for AutoBoost and the digital marketing executive identity. ACE freelance work should not surface here in any way that could read as Conley marketing himself for work outside his primary role.

**Strategy:**
- LinkedIn content focus remains: AutoBoost product updates, B2B marketing automation insights, digital marketing executive POV
- Do not post about ACE Phase I services, lead enrichment gigs, or Upwork activity
- If a Phase I client ever asks for a LinkedIn reference, handle case-by-case — but default to Upwork profile and portfolio site
- AutoBoost content cadence: 2–3 posts/week (unchanged from current)

**Estimated effort:** Already in-flight. No incremental effort allocated to this channel for ACE Phase I.

**Guardrail:** This separation is structural. AutoBoost = LinkedIn. ACE = everything else.

---

### Channel 5: Fiverr (Secondary Acquisition)

**Why this channel:** Fiverr serves a different buyer than Upwork — more price-sensitive, less scope-sophisticated, higher volume of smaller orders. Phase I doesn't prioritize Fiverr because Upwork's proposal system is better suited to the ACE pipeline. But a Fiverr gig is low-effort to stand up and can generate passive inbound leads once the gig ranks.

**Strategy:**
- Create one gig: "Verified B2B Lead List — 500 Contacts, Custom Targeting"
- Set at the Fiverr floor for this service category to get initial reviews (likely $150–250 for a starter package)
- Optimize the gig title and description for Fiverr search: "B2B lead list," "verified contacts," "targeted email list," "decision-maker data"
- Do not actively monitor Fiverr as a primary channel in Phase I — treat it as a passive acquisition mechanism

**Content format:** Gig title, description, FAQ, portfolio image (sample CSV preview)

**Estimated effort:** 2–3 hrs to set up (one-time), 30 min/week to monitor

**Success signal:** Gig live within first 2 weeks; first order within 30 days (stretch goal)

---

### Channel 6: Email (Retention and Re-engagement)

**Why this channel:** Phase I clients are low-volume but high-value. A single satisfied client who sends a referral or returns for a second list is worth 3–4 new Upwork applications. Email is how that relationship stays warm.

**Strategy:**
- Collect email from every client as part of the intake process (positioned as "I'll send delivery confirmation and follow-up support via email")
- Post-delivery sequence: 3 emails — Day 0 (delivery confirmation + check-in), Day 7 (did it work?), Day 30 (are you running another campaign? I have capacity)
- Keep emails short, direct, and written in Conley's voice — not a marketing newsletter, not a pitch
- If 5+ clients have been served, launch a quarterly re-engagement: one-line, no template, check-in on what they're working on next

**Content format:** Plain-text emails, no HTML template, no logo — just a person writing

**Estimated effort:** 1–2 hrs to build the sequence (one-time), 30 min/week to manage

**Success signal:** Every delivered client gets a Day 7 check-in; at least one returning client or referral within 90 days

## 9. Risks and Mitigations

**Risk 1: Upwork profile is too new to rank in search results**

New Upwork profiles have low visibility in organic search. Proposal-based outreach — not inbound profile discovery — will drive most early contracts.

*Mitigation:* Front-load proposal volume in weeks 2–4. The ACE pipeline is built for this. 10–15 proposals per week keeps the top-of-funnel full while the profile builds authority. Accept the first 2–3 contracts even at slightly below target rate to get the review flywheel turning.

---

**Risk 2: Bandwidth compression — fulfillment and marketing compete for the same 15 hrs/week**

As contracts close, fulfillment time increases. Marketing and content creation will feel like the first thing to cut.

*Mitigation:* Batch Threads content weekly, not daily. One writing session per week produces three posts. The email sequence is built in advance — it runs without active effort after setup. The portfolio site is a one-time build. Upwork proposals are pipeline-generated; manual review is 2 minutes per submission. None of these require constant attention — they require upfront setup and light weekly maintenance.

---

**Risk 3: Early review quality risk — a single 3-star review damages profile conversion at a critical moment**

Upwork reviews are public and disproportionately influential on a new profile. One bad review in the first five can tank conversion rate.

*Mitigation:* Apply Spartan Tone Protocol strictly on all proposals. Under-promise and over-deliver on the first 3 contracts. If a client seems unclear on scope before the contract starts, clarify in writing before accepting. If a contract is heading toward a negative outcome, proactively offer a revision or refund before the review is submitted — protecting the profile is worth more than the contract value in Phase I.
