# Writing Style — Conventions & Rules

**Related:** [[brand-voice]], [[second-brain-article]], [[portfolio-website]], [[building-out-loud]]

---

Voice and tone are handled in [[brand-voice]]. This page covers the craft layer underneath: mechanics, formatting conventions, and patterns to actively strike from any draft before it goes anywhere public.

---

## LLM-tells to eliminate on sight

These patterns are pervasive in AI-generated text. Humans have learned to recognize them visually. Using them signals that writing hasn't been meaningfully revised and undercuts whatever authenticity the content might otherwise have.

### Em dashes

No em dashes. Replace every instance with a comma, colon, semicolon, or period, depending on what the sentence actually needs. The substitution often forces a cleaner structure, which is the point. The variety that results (some sentences simpler, some more complex) is where the spontaneity and humanity come from.

Before: "It's not memory — it's a schema."
After: "A schema rather than memory, a way of making the model's behavior consistent."

Em dashes aren't categorically bad writing. LLMs just can't stop using them, and overuse has made them a visual tell. Treating them as off-limits is a useful heuristic, not a claim about grammar.

### "It's not X, it's Y"

The contrast pattern "it's not X, it's Y" (and the variant "it's not just X, it's Y") announces emphasis rather than creating it. AI defaults to this construction constantly.

Before: "It's not just bookkeeping. It's the continuity that makes sessions feel connected."
After: "The log is what keeps sessions from feeling disconnected, even weeks apart."

The rewrite usually finds a more direct path to the actual claim.

### Lists of three

LLMs default to groups of three because the rule of three is easy and has a natural rhythm. That's exactly why uniform three-item lists read as machine-generated. Vary them. Some lists genuinely have three items; that's fine. The problem is when every list in a piece comes in threes. Mix in twos, fours, fives. Let the content determine the length.

## Formatting conventions

### Bold headers

Never: `**bold text** —` followed by body text on the same line. This is recognizable LLM output.

Rarely: `**bold text:** body paragraph` on the same line as a repeating structural device. It can earn its place once in an article, in the right moment. It should not become the default way of organizing a section.

Even more rarely: `**bold:** body` inside a bulleted list. Reserve for cases where it adds something a plain bullet genuinely can't.

### Sentence fragments

Allowed. Direct, punchy, confident. Used when they serve voice. Not as a substitute for sentences that need to be sentences.

---

## Punctuation

No em dashes (see above). Everything else standard. After removing em dashes, punctuation variety tends to appear naturally because the em dash was doing most of the heavy lifting.

---

## Linking and attribution

Link to sources when the link adds value for the reader or supports a specific claim. External links to authoritative sources also support SEO. The question isn't whether to link in principle; it's whether a link in this specific spot is actually useful.

- Link people by name on first mention, pointing to their primary web presence
- Link tools and platforms on first mention
- Don't link for the sake of appearing thorough

Attribution: credit contributors by name and with a link wherever their work is referenced. Don't claim naming or inventing a concept without being certain of the history. When in doubt, say "what X calls Y" rather than "my implementation of Y."

## Positive targets

Variety in sentence length and structure. Spontaneity in how a paragraph moves. The occasional line that lands harder because of what came before it. Writing that reads like a person who has thought about something, not a system that has been asked to describe something.

See [[brand-voice]] for the specific voice attributes (calm ambition, grounded but hungry, technical but accessible, honest about the mess) and the language patterns that express them.
